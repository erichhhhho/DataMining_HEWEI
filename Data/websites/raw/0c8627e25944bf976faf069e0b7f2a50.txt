<h3 class="node-title lvl_44-title"><a href="/news/china/society/article/2117261/chinese-schools-told-cut-homework-parents-pick-slack" title="China’s schoolteachers told to go easy on the homework">China’s schoolteachers told to go easy on the homework</a></h3>
<time property="dc:modified" content="2017-10-27T12:45:31+08:00" datatype="xsd:dateTime" itemprop="dateModified" data-date-format="scmp_short" class="updated" datetime="2017-10-27T12:45:31+08:00">27 Oct 2017</time>
</div></article>

  
  
  </div>
    </div>
              <div class="col-xs-9 col-lg-9 pfcng-col pfcng-col-2">
          <div class="panel-pane pane-entity-field pane-node-body pane-first pos-0"  >
  
  
  
  
  <div class="pane-content">
    
  <p>A Chinese man has been sentenced to nine months in prison for making a joke on social media about joining Islamic State, local media reported.</p>
<p>The 31-year-old, identified only by the pseudonym Zhang Qiang, was found guilty of breaking China’s antiterrorism laws after suggesting to his friends in a private WeChat group that they should “come join ISIS with me”, using an abbreviation for the terrorist group, <em>Worker’s Daily</em> reported on Friday.</p>
<p>The incident happened on September 4 last year, after Zhang changed his profile picture to an image of the late al-Qaeda leader Osama bin Laden. On seeing the new picture, a friend messaged the group of about 300 people saying, “Look, a big shot just arrived.”</p>
<p><a href="http://www.scmp.com/news/china/policies-politics/article/2110806/wechat-users-censoring-content-amid-china-crackdown">WeChat users censoring content amid China crackdown on social media</a></p>
<p>Zhang, who lives in Beijing, responded to that comment with the message that landed him in prison.</p>
<p>He was arrested the following month and charged with spreading terrorist and extremist propaganda, though the investigation found no other terrorism-related comments on his phone or computer.</p>
<p>“It never crossed my mind that a joke would cause this much trouble for me,” he told reporters. “I really regret it.”</p>
<p>Zhang appeared in court in June. As well as the nine-month prison sentence, he was fined 1,000 yuan (US$150).</p>
<p>Many internet users argued that the punishment was too harsh, with one Jiangxi-based commentator saying: “Although his actions were bad, this sentence seems severe.”</p>
<p><a href="https://www.google.com/search?q=scmp+china+wechat+censor&amp;oq=scmp+china+wechat+censor&amp;gs_l=psy-ab.3..33i21k1j33i160k1.19094.22661.0.22902.21.18.1.0.0.0.128.910.17j1.18.0....0...1.1.64.psy-ab..4.1.128....0.eGwIaNbnS9Y">China’s WeChat crackdown drives bitcoin enthusiasts to Telegram</a></p>
<p>While China is not the first country to arrest someone for joking about terrorism online, several internet users questioned how Zhang’s comment became public knowledge considering it was made in a private WeChat group.</p>
<p>“Very curious about how the police found out,” a Guangdong-based user said.</p>
<p>The social media platform recently tightened its control over its users, making all group administrators criminally liable for messages posted in their chat rooms.<br /></p>
<p>An internet user from Beijing joked about reverting to more traditional forms of communication to avoid suffering the same fate as Zhang.</p>
<p>“I have already informed all my friends, in the future we will correspond by carrier pigeon, [and by] writing letters instead of calling each other. Only these ancient methods will be safe.”</p>
<p><a href="http://www.scmp.com/news/china/diplomacy-defence/article/2097608/china-gravely-concerned-report-two-chinese-killed">China ‘gravely concerned’ by report two Chinese killed by Islamic State in Pakistan</a></p>
<p>Beijing lawyer Shi Fumao was quoted by <em>Worker’s Daily</em> as saying that internet users should be careful about what they say online or “risk of committing a crime”.</p>
<p>In August, another Chinese person was sentenced to eight months in prison and fined 1,000 yuan for uploading a video to the internet that had terrorism-related content.</p>
