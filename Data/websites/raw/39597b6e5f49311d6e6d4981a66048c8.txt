<p class="byline">By <a href="http://www.pewresearch.org/author/lsilver/" title="View posts by Laura Silver" rel="author">Laura Silver</a></p>
<div class="text clearfix">
		<figure class="image-box alignleft has-caption"><a href="http://www.pewresearch.org/fact-tank/2017/10/16/how-people-in-asia-pacific-view-china/ft_17-10-13_china/" rel="attachment wp-att-295901"><img class="wp-photo size-full wp-image-295901" src="http://assets.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/12/2017/10/13110518/FT_17.10.13_china.jpg" alt="Many in Asia-Pacific countries say they are concerned about China's growing military power. (Xu Dongdong/VCG via Getty Images)" width="640" height="320" data-attachid="295901" /></a><div class="image-meta" style="max-width: 640px;"><span class="photo-caption">Many in Asia-Pacific countries say they are concerned about China&#8217;s growing military power. Here, a farewell ceremony for the fleet of China&#8217;s first aircraft carrier, Liaoning, on July 11 in Hong Kong. (Xu Dongdong/VCG via Getty Images)</div></figure>
<p>This week, China convenes its <a href="http://news.xinhuanet.com/english/2017-08/31/c_136571643.htm" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://news.xinhuanet.com/english/2017-08/31/c_136571643.htm', '19th National Congress');">19th National Congress</a>, a gathering closely watched by those seeking to understand power shifts and policy changes within the world’s most populous country. The meeting will feature a review of the Communist Party’s work over the past five years, including China’s <a href="http://news.xinhuanet.com/english/2017-08/29/c_136565429.htm" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://news.xinhuanet.com/english/2017-08/29/c_136565429.htm', 'foreign policy and its relations with its neighbors');">foreign policy and its relations with its neighbors</a>. Pew Research Center’s 2017 Global Attitudes Survey explored China’s image in the Asia-Pacific region. Here are four key findings about how China is viewed by its neighbors:</p>
<p> <big class="honkin-number" id="number-1">1</big><a class="image-box" href="http://www.pewresearch.org/fact-tank/2017/10/16/how-people-in-asia-pacific-view-china/ft_17-10-12_asiapacific_china_economy/" rel="attachment wp-att-295883"><img class="wp-photo size-full wp-image-295883 alignright" src="http://assets.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/12/2017/10/12161642/FT_17.10.12_AsiaPacific_China_economy.png" alt="" width="310" height="380" data-attachid="295883" /></a><strong>Views of China’s economic growth are mixed across the Asia-Pacific region. </strong>There is little consensus among the seven countries surveyed there about whether China’s growing economy is more of a good thing or a bad thing for their country. Three say the former, two the latter and two are nearly evenly split. Australians are most positive about China’s economic growth; by a three-to-one margin, more people say China’s economic growth is good for Australia than bad. In contrast, only 20% of Indians see China’s economic rise as a good thing for their country. The Asia-Pacific region is also one of the regions where most people name the U.S., not China, as the <a href="http://www.pewglobal.org/2017/07/13/more-name-u-s-than-china-as-worlds-leading-economic-power/" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.pewglobal.org/2017/07/13/more-name-u-s-than-china-as-worlds-leading-economic-power/', 'top global economic power');">top global economic power</a>.</p>
<p><span id="more-295887"></span></p>
<p> <big class="honkin-number" id="number-2">2</big><a class="image-box" href="http://www.pewresearch.org/fact-tank/2017/10/16/how-people-in-asia-pacific-view-china/ft_17-10-12_asiapacific_china_military/" rel="attachment wp-att-295884"><img class="wp-photo size-full wp-image-295884 alignright" src="http://assets.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/12/2017/10/12161643/FT_17.10.12_AsiaPacific_China_military.png" alt="" width="310" height="380" data-attachid="295884" /></a><strong>Most in the region worry about China’s growing military power. </strong>China’s <a href="https://www.defense.gov/Portals/1/Documents/pubs/2017_China_Military_Power_Report.PDF" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'https://www.defense.gov/Portals/1/Documents/pubs/2017_China_Military_Power_Report.PDF', 'official military budget');">official military budget</a> grew by about 9% per year over the past decade, and few of its neighbors appear to welcome the increase. In South Korea, Japan and Vietnam – countries actively engaged in disputes with China over <a href="https://www.reuters.com/article/us-northkorea-missiles-china-idUSKBN1AL0OW" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'https://www.reuters.com/article/us-northkorea-missiles-china-idUSKBN1AL0OW', 'military deployments');">military deployments</a> or territory in the <a href="https://www.cfr.org/interactives/chinas-maritime-disputes?cid=otr-marketing_use-china_sea_InfoGuide#!/chinas-maritime-disputes?cid=otr-marketing_use-china_sea_InfoGuide" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'https://www.cfr.org/interactives/chinas-maritime-disputes?cid=otr-marketing_use-china_sea_InfoGuide#!/chinas-maritime-disputes?cid=otr-marketing_use-china_sea_InfoGuide', 'East and South China seas');">East and South China seas</a> – nine-in-ten or more think China’s growing military power is a bad thing for their country. In India, even before a recent <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2017/08/28/world/asia/china-india-standoff-withdrawal.html?mcubz=1" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'https://www.nytimes.com/2017/08/28/world/asia/china-india-standoff-withdrawal.html?mcubz=1', 'border dispute');">border dispute</a> in the Himalayas, people had a negative view of Beijing by a more than three-to-one margin.</p>
<p> <big class="honkin-number" id="number-3">3</big><a class="image-box" href="http://www.pewresearch.org/fact-tank/2017/10/16/how-people-in-asia-pacific-view-china/ft_17-10-12_asiapacific_china_powerinfluence/" rel="attachment wp-att-295885"><img class="wp-photo size-full wp-image-295885 alignright" src="http://assets.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/12/2017/10/12161644/FT_17.10.12_AsiaPacific_China_powerInfluence.png" alt="" width="309" height="433" data-attachid="295885" /></a><strong>Although</strong> <strong>China’s power and influence is not seen as </strong><a href="http://www.pewglobal.org/2017/08/01/globally-people-point-to-isis-and-climate-change-as-leading-security-threats/#in-asia-pacific-views-of-top-threats-vary" onclick="__gaTracker('send', 'event', 'outbound-article', 'http://www.pewglobal.org/2017/08/01/globally-people-point-to-isis-and-climate-change-as-leading-security-threats/#in-asia-pacific-views-of-top-threats-vary', 'a top threat globally');"><strong>a top threat globally</strong></a><strong>, </strong><span class="s1"><b>many in Asia-Pacific </b></span><span class="s2"><b>countries</b></span><span class="s1"><b> see it a key concern.</b></span> Outside the region, a median of 27% see China’s power and influence as a major threat to their country. But among the seven Asia-Pacific countries surveyed, a median of 47% see it as a major threat. South Koreans and Vietnamese even rate China’s power and influence as the <em>top</em> threat facing their nations. Concerns about China also outpace concerns about the United States across the region; a median of 35% say U.S. power and influence is a major threat to their country.</p>
<p> <big class="honkin-number" id="number-4">4</big><a class="image-box" href="http://www.pewresearch.org/fact-tank/2017/10/16/how-people-in-asia-pacific-view-china/ft_17-10-12_asiapacific_china_xi/" rel="attachment wp-att-295886"><img class="wp-photo size-full wp-image-295886 alignright" src="http://assets.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/12/2017/10/12161645/FT_17.10.12_AsiaPacific_China_xi.png" alt="" width="420" height="413" data-attachid="295886" /></a><strong>Few in the region express confidence in Chinese President Xi Jinping.</strong> Most observers expect Xi to begin his second of two five-year terms at the 19th National Congress. Among China’s neighbors, however, there is limited confidence in him to do the right thing regarding world affairs. A median of 34% across the Asia-Pacific region say they have a lot or some confidence in him, ranging from a high of 53% in the Philippines to a low of 11% in Japan – a country where not even 1% say they have a lot of confidence in him. In India, Japan, Vietnam and the Philippines, Xi ranks behind U.S. President Donald Trump. Notably, however, in two U.S. treaty-allies in the region, Australia and South Korea, people have more confidence in Xi than in Trump.</p>
