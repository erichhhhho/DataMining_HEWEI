itemtype="https://schema.org/Person"><a href="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/en/author/fabio-nodari/" class="entry-author-link" itemprop="url" rel="author"><span class="entry-author-name" Comment</a></span></p><div class="ad-after-title"><section id="text-38" class="widget widget_text"><div class="widget-wrap"><div class="textwidget"><p><a href="https://www.airbnb.com/c/fabion3417"><img src="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/06/widgetairbnbhomebig-eng.jpg" alt="airbnb coupon" /></a></p></div></div></section></div></header><div class="entry-content" itemprop="text"><p><strong>Read about the efforts of The Nature Conservancy to protect the beautiful and endangered Black Snub-Nosed Monkey (also known as Yunnan Golden monkey).</strong></p><p><img src="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/yunnan-snub-nosed-monkey-750x500.jpg" alt="" width="750" height="500" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-14195" srcset="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/yunnan-snub-nosed-monkey-750x500.jpg 750w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/yunnan-snub-nosed-monkey-360x240.jpg 360w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/yunnan-snub-nosed-monkey-768x512.jpg 768w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/yunnan-snub-nosed-monkey-610x406.jpg 610w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/yunnan-snub-nosed-monkey.jpg 950w" sizes="(max-width: 750px) 100vw, 750px" /></p><p>Too often we read stories about species that go extinct even before the scientists can name or study them. Sadly today we live in a world where man has the power to destroy entire ecosystems all in the name of profits and power. <strong>There are however some stories with an happy ending,</strong> or at least whose ending doesn&#8217;t look as bad as in the past. The most incredible thing is that more and more of these stories come from a country that is notoriously famous for not doing much to protect our planet: <a href="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/en/china/" >China</a>. At least this is what happened for far too many years but, as I said, <strong>many things are changing even in China.</strong> I had the privilege to witness firsthand a small part of this change and I&#8217;ll talk about it in this post.<br /> But let me first briefly talk about some conservation success stories in China, what is <a href="https://www.nature.org/" rel="nofollow" >The Nature Conservancy</a> (from now on TNC) with <a href="https://www.tnc.org.hk/" rel="nofollow" >offices also in Hong Kong and China</a>, what they are doing and I&#8217;ll then talk about the beautiful <strong>Black Snub-Nosed Monkeys (also known as Yunnan Golden monkey).</strong></p><h2>Some conservation success stories in China</h2><p><img src="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/panda-china-750x499.jpg" alt="giant panda china" width="750" height="499" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-14197" srcset="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/panda-china-750x499.jpg 750w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/panda-china-360x239.jpg 360w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/panda-china-768x511.jpg 768w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/panda-china-610x406.jpg 610w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/panda-china.jpg 950w" sizes="(max-width: 750px) 100vw, 750px" /><br /> Among the conservation success stories the most famous is without a doubt about the <strong>Giant Panda.</strong> The first National Nature Reserve was established in Wolong in 1958 but back in those days not many people had the competence and knowledge to do the right thing. Many believed that the best method to protect the pandas was to cage them. In 1990s a better understanding of the species and several laws (including gun control and the removal of resident humans from the reserves) helped their chances of survival. Today there are <strong>almost 2000 in the wild</strong> and their number is rising.<br /> Everybody knows Pandas and everybody loves them. But this is not a coincidence. There is a specific reason why there are so many videos of cute Pandas. <strong>The best way to protect a species is to make the public love it.</strong> People who feel something for a species will be moved to make donations, protest when companies threatens their habitat, denounce poaching and so forth&#8230; This is exactly what happened whit Pandas. Of course the fact that Pandas are cute does help the cause, but the point is that when a species must be saved, people are very important.</p><blockquote><p>China <a href="https://www.quora.com/Does-China-legally-own-every-giant-panda-in-the-world-including-those-born-in-zoos-outside-China" rel="nofollow" >owns almost all the pandas in the world</a> and owns all future offspring of all pandas. The only exception are two pandas in Mexico.</p></blockquote><p>This works so well that today the Pandas are <a href="https://www.worldwildlife.org/stories/giant-panda-no-longer-endangered" rel="nofollow" >no longer endangered.</a> Of course this doesn&#8217;t mean that the Panda is save forever, but it&#8217;s definitely a great news.</p><p><img src="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/milu-deer-750x502.jpg" alt="milu deer" width="750" height="502" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-14198" srcset="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/milu-deer-750x502.jpg 750w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/milu-deer-360x241.jpg 360w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/milu-deer-768x514.jpg 768w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/milu-deer-610x408.jpg 610w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/milu-deer.jpg 950w" sizes="(max-width: 750px) 100vw, 750px" /><br /> Another story with an happy ending is about the <strong>Milu Deer or Père David&#8217;s deer.</strong> This species of deer has long been famed as having the head of a horse, the hooves of a cow, the tail of a donkey and the antlers of a deer. How the species was spared from extinction is an unlikely tale spanning lifetimes and involving a French missionary, an English duke and a Slovak-born American zoologist.<br /> This is pretty much what happened and you can read more about it <a href="http://www.chinapost.com.tw/life/environment/2017/04/27/496292/In-conservation.htm" rel="nofollow" >here</a> and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P%C3%A8re_David%27s_deer" rel="nofollow" >here</a>: In the late 19th century, the world&#8217;s only herd belonged to Tongzhi, the Emperor of China. The herd was maintained in the Nanyuan Royal Hunting Garden in Nan Haizi, near Peking. In 1895, one of the walls of the hunting garden was destroyed by a heavy flood of the Yongding River, and most of the deer escaped and were killed and eaten by starving peasants. Fewer than thirty Père David&#8217;s Deer remained in the garden. Then in 1900, during the Boxer Rebellion, the garden was occupied by troops and the remaining deer were shot and eaten, leaving the <strong>Père David&#8217;s deer extinct in its native China.</strong><br /> A few of the deer had previously been illegally transported to Europe for exhibition and breeding. After the extirpation of the Chinese population in 1900, Herbrand Russell, 11th Duke of Bedford, was instrumental in saving the species. He acquired the few remaining animals from European zoos and nurtured a herd at Woburn Abbey. Reintroduction of Père David&#8217;s deer to China  began in 1985 in the spot where they had last been seen alive in their native country. This was followed in 1986 and 1987 by a second and third herd. Today, there are about <strong>5,500 milu deer in China,</strong> with as many as 600 living in the wild in Hubei and Hunan provinces along the Yangtze River. The current world population, stems from the Woburn Abbey herd. This is quite an incredible story!<br /> Finally you can read about <a href="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/en/siberian-tiger-china-new-national-park/" >another good news</a> in this my recent post.</p><h2>What is The Nature Conservancy</h2><p><img src="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/thenatureconservancy-750x228.jpg" alt="the nature conservancy" width="750" height="228" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-14201" srcset="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/thenatureconservancy-750x228.jpg 750w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/thenatureconservancy-360x110.jpg 360w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/thenatureconservancy-768x234.jpg 768w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/thenatureconservancy-610x186.jpg 610w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/thenatureconservancy.jpg 950w" sizes="(max-width: 750px) 100vw, 750px" /><br /> <a href="https://www.nature.org/" rel="nofollow" >The Nature Conservancy (TNC)</a> is a charitable environmental organization, based in Arlington, Virginia, United States. Its mission is to &#8220;conserve the lands and waters on which all life depends.&#8221; Their work focuses on the global priorities of Lands, Water, Climate, Oceans, and Cities. It was founded in 1951 and now impacts conservation in 69 countries. It has <strong>over one million members, and has protected more than 48,000,000 ha of land and thousands of miles of rivers worldwide.</strong> TNC also operates more than 100 marine conservation projects globally and is the largest environmental nonprofit by assets and by revenue in the Americas. TNC rates as one of the most trusted national organizations. Some of the members include former U.S. Senator Bill Frist, chairman of the Alibaba Group Jack Ma, and Chairman and Co-founder of The Bridgespan Group Thomas Tierney.<br /> This introduction is very important because <strong>I&#8217;m sure you know WWF but maybe you have never heard of TNC.</strong> However they are very important and where the first foreign NGO devoted to protect the environment who was allowed to work in China. They are actually so well known and trusted by the Chinese government that often, when a new natural reserve has to be established, the local authorities contact TNC for a consulence.<br /> Recently I had the chance to meet several members of TNC working on different project in China (<a href="https://www.tnc.org.hk/" rel="nofollow" >through TNC Hong Kong</a>)and I asked them few questions about the challenges of working in this unique country. What I understood was that many things are changing. Even if there are still quite a lot of problems, more and more people (especially the younger generations) are becoming aware of the importance of protecting and respecting the planet we live in. China is going through an industrial revolution and like what happened in Europe, nature conservancy hasn&#8217;t been of the top of the list of important things to do. But because of this economic boom people are now more educated, have more money to spend and more time to travel. So they slowly begin to appreciate the beauty of the nature and the importance of protecting it. <strong>There is still a long way to go but at least the first steps towards the right direction have been taken.</strong><br /> This introduction brings me back to the main topic of this post: <strong>the Black Snub-Nosed Monkey.</strong></p><h2>Black Snub-Nosed Monkey: a conservation success story by TNC</h2><p><img src="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/male-yunnan-black-snub-nosed-monkey1-750x500.jpg" alt="male yunnan black snub nosed monkey" width="750" height="500" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-14204" srcset="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/male-yunnan-black-snub-nosed-monkey1-750x500.jpg 750w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/male-yunnan-black-snub-nosed-monkey1-360x240.jpg 360w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/male-yunnan-black-snub-nosed-monkey1-768x512.jpg 768w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/male-yunnan-black-snub-nosed-monkey1-610x406.jpg 610w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/male-yunnan-black-snub-nosed-monkey1.jpg 950w" sizes="(max-width: 750px) 100vw, 750px" /><br /> Let&#8217;s face it: the Black Snub-Nosed Monkeys <strong>are not the cutest animals you have ever seen.</strong> Aren&#8217;t they? First they don&#8217;t have the nose (in case you haven&#8217;t noticed it) second they have a really weird &#8220;Elvis-like-hairstyle&#8221;. <strong>But I love them anyway and I DO believe they are beautiful!</strong> These features is what makes them unique and immediately recognizable even by someone who doesn&#8217;t know much about wildlife. These animals are unique also in many other aspects.</p><h2>Black Snub-Nosed Monkey facts</h2><p><img src="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/baby-snub-nosed-monkey-750x500.jpg" alt="" width="750" height="500" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-14205" srcset="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/baby-snub-nosed-monkey-750x500.jpg 750w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/baby-snub-nosed-monkey-360x240.jpg 360w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/baby-snub-nosed-monkey-768x512.jpg 768w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/baby-snub-nosed-monkey-610x406.jpg 610w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/baby-snub-nosed-monkey.jpg 950w" sizes="(max-width: 750px) 100vw, 750px" /></p><p>These are some interesting facts about the Black Snub-Nosed Monkey:</p><li>It was discovered by scientists in the 1890s, then lost and thought to be extinct until 1962.</li><li>The Black Snub-Nosed Monkey was almost completely unknown by the scientific community until the 1990s.</li><li>They make their home at altitudes higher than any other primate except humans. They can survive at an altitude of up to 4700 meters.</li><li>The Black Snub-Nosed Monkey lives in very large super-groups which are made up of several families. Each of them has a single dominant male with many females.</li><li>Their favorite food is the Spanish moss that grows abundantly in the remote forests of South China but it takes a lot to reform. So the monkeys move fast looking for new sources of food.</li><li>The Black Snub-Nosed Monkeys (Rhinopithecus bieti &#8211; 黑金丝猴), native of Yunnan, are not the same as the Golden Snub-Nosed Monkeys (Rhinopithecus roxellana &#8211; 川金丝猴) that are native of SiChuan. The Black Snub-Nosed Monkeys left in the wild are about 3000 while the Golden Snub-Nosed Monkeys range from 8,000 to 15,000. The Tonkin Snub-Nosed Monkey is yet another species that lives in northern Vietnam.</li><li>Due to their particular nose, when it rains they sneeze quite often.</li><li>They give birth about once every two or three years.</li><li>The main danger for this species are habitat loss (due to the illegal logging and removal of dead trees) and illegal hunting.</li><li>The Black Snub-Nosed Monkeys are mainly found in the BaiMa Snow Mountain National Park (白马雪山).</li><p></br><br /> <img src="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/yunnan-snub-nosed-monkey-habitat-750x500.jpg" alt="Snub-Nosed Monkey habitat" width="750" height="500" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-14208" srcset="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/yunnan-snub-nosed-monkey-habitat-750x500.jpg 750w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/yunnan-snub-nosed-monkey-habitat-360x240.jpg 360w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/yunnan-snub-nosed-monkey-habitat-768x512.jpg 768w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/yunnan-snub-nosed-monkey-habitat-610x406.jpg 610w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/yunnan-snub-nosed-monkey-habitat.jpg 950w" sizes="(max-width: 750px) 100vw, 750px" /><em>The Black Snub-Nosed Monkey lives in these remote forests in Southern China.</em></p><p>What is the role played by TNC in saving the Black Snub-Nosed Monkey? <a href="https://www.nature.org/ourinitiatives/regions/asiaandthepacific/china/explore/saving-a-mysterious-monkey.xml" rel="nofollow" >This article pretty much explains everything.</a> Long story short, the <a href="https://www.nature.org/ourinitiatives/regions/asiaandthepacific/china/explore/the-nature-conservancy-in-china-dr-long-yongcheng.xml" rel="nofollow" >chinese biologist Long Yongcheng</a> spent <strong>over 10 years</strong> searching for the beautiful Yunnan Black Snub-Nosed Monkeys in the wild. Equipped with just a telescope and notebook — no tent, no camping supplies — Long trekked into the thick alpine forests alone, leaving only to seek food from local herders and farmers. It took him four weeks to catch his first glimpse of live monkeys. Sadly he got very little support. No one cared about his researches. He almost gave up but then finally in 2001 he found <strong>the right partner: <a href="https://www.tnc.org.hk/" rel="nofollow" >TNC.</a></strong> Since then, everything changed. <strong>The number tripled from 1000 to 3000 and it keeps rising.</strong></p><h2>Where can you see and photograph the Black Snub-Nosed Monkeys?</h2><p><img src="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/baby-snub-nosed-monkey-1-750x500.jpg" alt="" width="750" height="500" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-14211" srcset="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/baby-snub-nosed-monkey-1-750x500.jpg 750w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/baby-snub-nosed-monkey-1-360x240.jpg 360w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/baby-snub-nosed-monkey-1-768x512.jpg 768w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/baby-snub-nosed-monkey-1-610x406.jpg 610w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/baby-snub-nosed-monkey-1.jpg 950w" sizes="(max-width: 750px) 100vw, 750px" /><br /> There is another good news: if you want to see the Black Snub-Nosed Monkey in the wild <strong>it&#8217;s possible to do so</strong> and you don&#8217;t have to spend 10 years looking for them. 🙂 You&#8217;ll have to get to TaCheng (塔城) about 4 hours by car from Lijiang, in Yunnan. Getting to the natural reserve though is not easy since there is no public transportation and you only have the option to arrange a tour with a guide. I recommend the<a href="http://wondersofyunnan.com/" rel="nofollow" > wondersofyunnan travel agency</a> since they can arrange for an English speaking guide and have been working in China for a long time and are a trusted company (tell them I gave you their contact).<br /> Once in the park you&#8217;ll have to pay the usual <strong>100RMB fee</strong> to get inside (one ticket is valid only for one day if you want to watch the monkeys). There are very few accommodation options: last time I checked <a href="http://www.booking.com/searchresults.xu.html?landmark=900058478&#038;aid=1128350&#038;no_rooms=1&#038;group_adults=2&#038;room1=A%2CA" rel="nofollow" >Booking.com</a> I didn&#8217;t find any hotel. But I saw some when I was there. I stayed in a very nice <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lisu_people" rel="nofollow" >Lisu</a> (the name of the minority living in the area) traditional house.<br /> <img src="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/lisu-traditional-house-750x167.jpg" alt="" width="750" height="167" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-14213" srcset="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/lisu-traditional-house-750x167.jpg 750w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/lisu-traditional-house-360x80.jpg 360w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/lisu-traditional-house-768x171.jpg 768w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/lisu-traditional-house-610x135.jpg 610w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/lisu-traditional-house.jpg 950w" sizes="(max-width: 750px) 100vw, 750px" /><br /> It&#8217;s very simple (it doesn&#8217;t have heating system even if the location is 2500meters high!) but very nice, built with wood and stone. I loved it.</p><p>So why is it possible to see the monkeys in TaCheng? <strong>A particularly harsh winter forced the starving Snub-Nosed Monkeys closer to the village</strong> and since then a group of rangers feed the monkeys regularly, thus making this group not entirely wild. They are free to come and go whenever they like but obviously they tend to stick around. Some people will jump to the conclusion that interfering with the life of these monkeys is not good, but honestly I don&#8217;t agree. As I said with the case of the pandas, to be successful in preserving a species people must love it. To love a species people have to see it and seeing the monkeys in this environment is definitely better than any zoo. The scientists decided to sacrifice, so to say, this group, for the sake of the whole species. sizes="(max-width: 750px) 100vw, 750px" /></div></figure></div><h2>What&#8217;s the key to successfully protect endangered species in China?</h2><p><iframe width="750" height="422" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/ZX4LUXewQxY?list=PLQhFtWO8Xkt7iLxYrmC-VIlZz1Msp-zbm" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen></iframe></p><p><strong>Establishing a National Park is not enough to protect a species.</strong> This because most of the time these wild animals live in the same areas where very poor people live. They hunt the animals and destroy the environment but before we judge them let&#8217;s keep in mind that this poor people are completely uneducated about environment conservation and do what they do not because of some sadistic reason. They need food and wood in their daily lives. Plus most of them are old and believe in traditional Chinese medicines that teaches that animal bones have some healing powers. It&#8217;s really hard to eradicate those believes. The only lasting solution is to involve the local community. <strong>Make them part of the conservation effort. Create new jobs</strong> like rangers, guest houses and so forth so that people won&#8217;t have any reason to threaten the wildlife and the environment. Raise awareness and help hunters switch to other livelihoods. For example some hunters are switching to farming. In one Conservancy-funded pilot project, a former hunter is now growing morel mushrooms, making far more money than before.</p><p><img src="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/chinese-rangers-750x500.jpg" alt="chinese rangers" width="750" height="500" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-14216" srcset="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/chinese-rangers-750x500.jpg 750w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/chinese-rangers-360x240.jpg 360w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/chinese-rangers-768x512.jpg 768w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/chinese-rangers-610x406.jpg 610w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/chinese-rangers.jpg 950w" sizes="(max-width: 750px) 100vw, 750px" /><em>Chinese rangers who were once hunters and farmers, thus threatening the environment, are now involved in protecting the Snub-Nosed Monkeys</em></p><p>Last but not least: <strong>educate the younger generations.</strong> They are the future. The new generations are the ones who can save endangered species and organisations like TNC are doing a great job educating them.<br /> This story is just one of the many and, at once, this is a story with an happy ending. The futures seems to be a little bit bright even in China at least for the Black Snub-Nosed Monkeys.</p><p><img src="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/snub-nosed-monkey-yunnan-750x500.jpg" alt="" width="750" height="500" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-14218" srcset="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/snub-nosed-monkey-yunnan-750x500.jpg 750w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/snub-nosed-monkey-yunnan-360x240.jpg 360w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/snub-nosed-monkey-yunnan-768x512.jpg 768w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/snub-nosed-monkey-yunnan-610x406.jpg 610w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/snub-nosed-monkey-yunnan.jpg 950w" sizes="(max-width: 750px) 100vw, 750px" /></p><p>You can also play a role in the Conservancy&#8217;s efforts to save the Black Snub-Nosed Monkey when you <a href="https://secure.tnc.org.hk/page/contribute/donate_recurring_en" rel="nofollow" >support TNC work.</a></p><p><strong>P.S.</strong><br /> A big thanks to <strong>TNC staff in Lijiang and Heqing</strong> who is working so hard and passionately to protect the Wild Wonders of China: well done! A big thanks also to Michele of the Hong Kong staff of TNC. Don&#8217;t forget to follow <a href="https://www.instagram.com/tnc_hk/" rel="nofollow" >their Instagram page.</a></p><p><img src="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/tnc-stuff-lijiang-750x167.jpg" alt="" width="750" height="167" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-14217" srcset="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/tnc-stuff-lijiang-750x167.jpg 750w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/tnc-stuff-lijiang-360x80.jpg 360w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/tnc-stuff-lijiang-768x171.jpg 768w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/tnc-stuff-lijiang-610x135.jpg 610w, https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/tnc-stuff-lijiang.jpg 950w" sizes="(max-width: 750px) 100vw, 750px" /></p><h2>Reminders for your trip to Southern China:</h2><li><a href="https://www.fabionodariphoto.com/en/best-vpn-china/" >Get a VPN before you arrive in China. </a>Internet is censored and you won&#8217;t be able to use Gmail, Google, Facebook, ecc without it.
