		<h3>å®ä¼èå¾<br>Think big</h3> 

		<p>It might be unnerving for Western nations to see a newcomer mastering an important technology, especially when the full potential of that technology remains uncertain. But it is wrong to view this story simply in terms of competition with the West.</p> 

		<p>A big problem facing both the U.S. and China is slowing economic growth. While AI may eliminate certain jobs, it also has the potential to greatly expand the economy and create wealth by making many industries far more efficient and productive. China has embraced that simple fact more eagerly and more completely than many Western nations. But thereâs no reason why Chinaâs AI-fueled economic progress should come at the expense of other countries, if those countries embrace the same technology just as keenly.</p> 

		<p>China might have unparalleled resources and enormous untapped potential, but the West has world-Â­leading expertise and a strong research culture. Rather than worry about Chinaâs progress, it would be wise for Western nations to focus on their existing strengths, investing heavily in research and education. The risk is missing out on an incredibly important technological shift. Yes, companies like Google and Facebook are making important strides in AI today, but this isnât enough to reboot a whole economy. Despite the fanfare around AI, there are few economic signsâsuch as increased productivityâthat most of the economy is taking advantage of the technology yet. Large segments of the economy beyond Silicon Valley, like medicine, service industries, and manufacturing, also need to sign on.</p> 

		<p>I canât help thinking of the poker tournament in Hainan and reflecting that the rest of the world should take inspiration from Lengpudashi, the poker-playing AI. Itâs time to follow Chinaâs lead and go all in on artificial intelligence.</p>
