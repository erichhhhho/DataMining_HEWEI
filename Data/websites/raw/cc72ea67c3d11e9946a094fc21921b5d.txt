<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="303" data-total-count="2277">“Party leaders always feel peril close at hand, especially Xi, and that has not gone away,” said Deng Yuwen, a former editor with a Communist Party journal who now writes current affairs commentaries. “For him, this hard-line, centralized style of rule is the solution and must be consolidated.”</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="341" data-total-count="2618">While Mao promoted class struggle and Deng Xiaoping embraced pragmatic capitalism, Mr. Xi’s vision of the party’s rule centers on restoring China to greatness — what he calls the “China Dream” — and it draws on both the fervent dedication of Mao’s era and the glories of China’s traditional culture that Mao tried to destroy.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="234" data-total-count="2852">In practice, that has meant a campaign to impose greater discipline in the party’s ranks, and political repression outside the party, including a crackdown on activists and more stringent media censorship, including on the internet.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="392" data-total-count="3244">“Under Xi Jinping, the Chinese Communist Party is headed in the direction of strongman rule,” said <a href="https://www.sais-jhu.edu/david-lampton">David M. Lampton</a>, the director of China studies at the Johns Hopkins School of Advanced International Studies and a longtime analyst of Chinese leaders. “The 19th Party Congress is more likely to look like a coronation than an institutionalized transition to a leader’s second term.”</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="149" data-total-count="3393">One big question as the congress got underway was whether Mr. Xi would try to claim an even bigger role than he already has in the nation’s future.</p><figure class="media video youtube embedded layout-large-horizontal">
        <iframe class="video-bind" width="420" height="315" src="//youtube.com/embed/c3C_johrfpk?wmode=transparent" allowfullscreen></iframe>

</figure>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="221" data-total-count="3614">If he follows the script for leadership successions that his predecessors have followed since the 1990s, Mr. Xi will promote some next-generation leaders, who would be waiting in the wings for him to retire in five years.</p><div id="story-ad-2" class="story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent ">

<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="203" data-total-count="3817" id="story-continues-5">Such figures could include Chen Min’er, the party secretary of Chongqing in southwest China, who was close to Mr. Xi when they were both provincial officials and <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2017/09/12/world/asia/china-xi-jinping-successor-chen-miner.html">enjoyed Mr. Xi’s conspicuous support</a>.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="152" data-total-count="3969">But Mr. Xi may instead open the way for holding onto power in some form beyond his second term, breaking with the practice of his immediate predecessor.</p><figure id="media-100000005499901" class="media photo embedded layout-large-horizontal media-100000005499901 ratio-tall" data-media-action="modal" itemprop="associatedMedia" itemscope itemid="https://static01.nyt.com/images/2017/10/18/world/18china-party-3/18china-party-3-master675.jpg" itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject" aria-label="media" role="group">

<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="163" data-total-count="4132">One way he could do this would be to <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2016/10/05/world/asia/china-president-xi-jinping-successor.html">hold off in appointing</a> any promising younger leaders to the Politburo Standing Committee, the party’s highest rung of power.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="109" data-total-count="4241">Another would be to enable his ally and anti-corruption czar, Wang Qishan, to stay in the Standing Committee.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="286" data-total-count="4527">At age 69, Mr. Wang has exceeded the age, 68, when an informal party rule says top officials should step down once the next party congress comes around. Keeping Mr. Wang on could set a precedent for Mr. Xi to buck the same rule at the end of his second term, when he will be 69 himself.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="218" data-total-count="4745">Mr. Xi also appears likely to use this congress to mark his place in the party’s history by inscribing his ideas, and perhaps even his name, into the party’s constitution, giving his policies a sheen of permanence.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="381" data-total-count="5126">“He’s going to get the ideological canonization,” said <a href="https://www.csis.org/people/christopher-k-johnson">Christopher K. Johnson</a>, a China expert at the Center for Strategic and International Studies and a former analyst at the Central Intelligence Agency. “If you’re opposing him, that’s no longer just a disagreement with the individual party chief or Xi Jinping personally. Then you’re challenging the party line.”</p><div id="story-ad-3" class="story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent ">

<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="237" data-total-count="5363" id="story-continues-6">The weeklong congress in Beijing will be a carefully choreographed celebration of Communist rule, complete with the pageantry of goose-stepping soldiers, fluttering red hammer-and-sickle flags and the playing of “The Internationale.”</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="290" data-total-count="5653">Yet despite the triumphant stagecraft, Mr. Xi remains driven by a fear that Communist rule could collapse in China as it did in the Soviet Union unless the party maintains firm control over an increasingly wealthy and diverse society that now has <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2017/10/12/business/china-richest-billionaires.html">over a third of the world’s billionaires</a>.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="255" data-total-count="5908">To do this, Mr. Xi has tightened his control over possible competing centers of power, <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2017/08/01/business/dalian-wanda-group-china-debt.html">including </a><a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2017/08/01/business/dalian-wanda-group-china-debt.html">those billionaires</a> and their businesses, the internet, <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2017/10/11/world/asia/xi-jinping-military-china-purge.html">the military</a> and other arms of state power, as well as over the 89 million members of the party itself.</p><figure id="media-100000005499920" class="media photo embedded layout-small-vertical media-100000005499920" data-media-action="modal" itemprop="associatedMedia" itemscope itemid="https://static01.nyt.com/images/2017/10/18/world/18china-party-4/17china-party-4-master180.jpg" itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject" aria-label="media" role="group">

<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="185" data-total-count="6093">Mr. Xi’s opening address on Wednesday morning was crafted to sound a warning that the party cannot let down its guard, said analysts and party insiders who have followed preparations.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="198" data-total-count="6291">“Treating development and security in tandem, enhancing a sense of peril, remaining vigilant in times of peace — this is a major principle of our party’s governance of the country,” he said.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="282" data-total-count="6573">Mr. Xi has also called for renewed commitment to what he calls “reform” and did so again in his speech to the congress. But his notion of reform is unlikely to look much like previous bouts of economic liberalization that Deng and other leaders unleashed in the 1980s and 1990s.</p><div class="newsletter-signup hidden auto-newsletter variant-1-hidden " id="newsletter-promo" data-newsletter-productCode="" data-newsletter-productTitle="" aria-labeledby="newsletter-promo-heading">

It disciplines the party, it disciplines Chinese society. And to enforce discipline, you have to have a very powerful security state.”</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="262" data-total-count="7695">More than any other recent Chinese leader, Mr. Xi has cast himself as the salvation of both party and nation. He has also been dismissive of his recent predecessors, whom he implicitly blames for failing to act forcefully enough to ensure the party’s survival.</p><figure id="media-100000005499921" class="media photo embedded layout-small-vertical media-100000005499921" data-media-action="modal" itemprop="associatedMedia" itemscope itemid="https://static01.nyt.com/images/2017/10/18/world/18china-party-5/17china-party-5-master180.jpg" itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject" aria-label="media" role="group">

<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="121" data-total-count="7816">For Mr. Xi, the fall of the Soviet Union is a warning of what will go wrong if China’s party succumbs to those dangers.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="226" data-total-count="8042">“Why did the Soviet Union collapse?” Mr. Xi <a href="http://theory.people.com.cn/n1/2016/0421/c40531-28292899.html">said to officials in 2013</a>. “There was ideological chaos, the party apparatus at every level seemed ineffectual, the military was no longer under the leadership of the party.”</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="301" data-total-count="8343">Mr. Xi wants to ensure that the Chinese Communist Party stays in power long after it celebrates the centenary of its founding four years from now, and long after it overtakes the Communist Party of the Soviet Union in length of time in power, a milestone that by some measures it will cross next year.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="346" data-total-count="8689">As the “princeling” son of a revolutionary, Mr. Xi exudes a sense of inherited responsibility for preserving the party. Since he took office, Mr. Xi has removed officials for corruption and disloyalty more senior than previous leaders dared take on, and overseen investigations of <a href="http://news.xinhuanet.com/english/2017-09/17/c_136615871.htm">more than 200 officials</a> at the vice-minister level or higher.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="356" data-total-count="9045">Mr. Xi has also ordered party members and officials to return to an almost puritan faith in Marxism and Mao, even amid China’s flashy wealth. He introduced draconian rules weaning cadres <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2013/03/28/world/asia/xi-jinping-imposes-austerity-measures-on-chinas-elite.html">off fancy meals</a>, heavy drinking and <a href="https://www.wsj.com/articles/at-chinas-npc-karaoke-goes-quiet-as-austerity-takes-over-1426187763">karaoke sessions</a>. He shook up the military and security services, dumping dozens of generals, including two commanders last summer.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="246" data-total-count="9291">The party was beset by “laxity and softness” before he took charge, Mr. Xi told the delegates on Wednesday. Now, he continued, “the overwhelming force of the anti-corruption struggle has coalesced and is being consolidated and developed.”</p><div id="story-ad-5" class="story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent ">

<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="131" data-total-count="9422" id="story-continues-8">He has also created a plethora of new leadership groups that allow him to control policy more directly than previous party leaders.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="246" data-total-count="9668">“Xi started off fast,” said <a href="http://www.bu.edu/polisci/people/faculty/fewsmith/">Joseph Fewsmith</a>, a professor at Boston University who studies Chinese elite politics. “Now he looks like he’s got all the pieces in place and will start whatever the positive agenda of the Xi Jinping era is.”</p><button class="button comments-button  theme-speech-bubble-large" data-skip-to-para-id="">

<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="120" data-total-count="9788">The major decisions to be unveiled this week were most likely made in advance by a small circle of senior party leaders.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="248" data-total-count="10036">“The intense interest in this congress is how far Xi can and will go in reshaping the norms of Chinese politics to get his way,” Professor Fewsmith said. “Everyone has been on pins and needles because they sense that something is changing.”</p><footer class="story-footer story-content">
