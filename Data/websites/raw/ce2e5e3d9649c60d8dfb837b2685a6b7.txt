It is not all about the money &#8211; it is about following our passion.&#8221;</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h4><strong>CHINA&#8217;S PRICELESS CHILDREN</strong></h4><blockquote><p><em><span style="font-size: 35px;">&#8220;Around 8 million university graduates enter China&#8217;s job market each year.&#8221;</span></em></p></blockquote><p>&nbsp;</p><p>The high job expectations among China&#8217;s post-90s workers relate to their position in their families and in society at large. As described by Liu Fengshu (2015) in &#8220;The Rise of the &#8216;Priceless&#8217; Child in China,&#8221; the so-called <em>jiulinghou</em> is a one-child generation that was born amidst the rapid socio-economic changes of post-Mao China, which saw a dramatic growth of both wealth and technological developments.</p><p>Parents and grandparents have both pampered and pressured these children; not just because it takes more than a high school education to succeed in a society that is changing so quickly, but also because the urban post-90s generation was the first to have access to education and career opportunities in a way their (grand)parents never had. They are therefore also often called the &#8216;lucky generation&#8217; (å¹¸è¿çä¸ä»£).</p><div id="attachment_15480" style="width: 460px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img class="size-full wp-image-15480" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODdhAQABAPAAAP///wAAACwAAAAAAQABAEACAkQBADs=" data-lazy-src="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/s4_5462f2c280cad.jpg" alt="" width="450" height="290" data-lazy-srcset="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/s4_5462f2c280cad.jpg 450w, https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/s4_5462f2c280cad-300x193.jpg 300w" data-lazy-sizes="(max-width: 450px) 100vw, 450px" /><noscript><img class="size-full wp-image-15480" src="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/s4_5462f2c280cad.jpg" alt="" width="450" height="290" srcset="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/s4_5462f2c280cad.jpg 450w, https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/s4_5462f2c280cad-300x193.jpg 300w" sizes="(max-width: 450px) 100vw, 450px" /></noscript><p class="wp-caption-text">Editorial cartoon from Chinese media. Around 8 million new university graduates now enter China&#8217;s job market each year, but their chances of finding a job that suits their education and personal expectations are slim.</p><p>With all their high hopes and graduate diplomas, they are facing a job market mismatch. They often have no working experience and, as they have often spent years studying before entering the employment market, they are not willing to take on jobs with low education requirements.</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h4><strong>&#8216;LUCKY&#8217; BUT STRESSED</strong></h4><blockquote><p><span style="font-size: 35px;"><em>&#8220;If any of these requirements are not there, it will be very stressful for us.&#8221;</em></span></p></blockquote><p>&nbsp;</p><p>Is the <em>jiulinghou</em> being unreasonable in what they expect from their work? A Beijing post-90er nicknamed &#8216;Pedy&#8217;, who teaches Chinese to foreigners at a Chaoyang educational institution, does not think so. She tells What&#8217;s on Weibo: &#8220;We all just want a job that (1) we find interesting, (2) suits our skills, (3) offers a reasonable salary, (4) is located not too far from where we live, and (5) gives us a sense of success or achievement. If any of these requirements are not there, it will be very stressful for us.&#8221;</p><p>&#8216;Pedy&#8217; mentions the cost of life in China&#8217;s bigger cities as one of the major problems: &#8220;The salaries of people just starting out on the job market are generally quite low, while the cost of living in cities like Beijing is very high. But if you live too far from your company, it means spending long and tiring hours crammed in public transport every morning and every evening.&#8221;</p><p><img class="aligncenter size-large their possibilities, or to hop from company to company until they find what they are looking for. Unafraid of losing a job they do not care much for anyway, many of these post-90ers are those who have become known for quitting their job because of the &#8216;cold weather&#8217; or other seemingly random reasons.</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h4><strong>THE SO-CALLED &#8216;DREAM JOB&#8217;</strong></h4><blockquote><p><em><span style="font-size: 35px;">&#8220;If we do not finish our projects in time, they will subtract an amount from our wages.&#8221;</span></em></p></blockquote><p>&nbsp;</p><p>Perhaps the biggest challenge faced by China&#8217;s well-educated, urban post-90s workers who are entering the employment market is the troubling Catch 22 situation at hand: they will be stressed and pressured if they do not find that top job, but when they do, they are often also stressed and pressured.</p><p>When scoring a much-desired job at one of the top companies (such as one of the &#8216;Big Four&#8217; firms Deloitte, Ernst &#038; Young, PricewaterhouseCoopers/PwC and KPMG), young workers will often do all they can to keep their job. These jobs come with relatively high salaries and future possibilities to higher positions, but they also go hand in hand with long hours and unpaid overwork. Those who refuse to work overtime will be labeled as &#8216;non-ambitious&#8217; or &#8216;not loyal enough.&#8217;</p><p>Beijing resident Li Jiang has a good job that suits his skills, is not too far from home, has a good salary, and offers prospects for further growth. Despite his stable contract, he is used to working over hours and often does not come home until late at night.</p><p>&#8220;We do not get paid for overtime,&#8221; Li says: &#8220;But they just give us too much work to handle. If we do not finish our projects in time, they will subtract an amount from our wages, although this is not noted in my contract.&#8221;</p><p>Although (illegal) overtime may endanger workers&#8217; health due to the excessive long working hours, it is still commonplace in China (Kim &#038; Chung 2016). Nobody wants to be the one leaving first or sticking their heads out to ask about employee rights. <em>Meiyoubanfa</em>, it&#8217;s just the way it is.&#8221;</p><p>Yue Xin from Shanghai has something different in mind for her future. She has received job offers from several companies, but despite &#8220;feeling flattered,&#8221; none of them met her expectations. An essay titled &#8220;I Am Fan Yusu&#8221; (&#8220;<a href="http://hx.cnd.org/2017/04/29/139522/" target="_blank">ææ¯èé¨ç´ </a>,&#8221; <u><em><a href="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/fan-yusu-%e6%88%91%e6%98%af%e8%8c%83%e9%9b%a8%e7%b4%a0-full-translation/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">full translation </a><a href="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/fan-yusu-%e6%88%91%e6%98%af%e8%8c%83%e9%9b%a8%e7%b4%a0-full-translation/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">here</a></em></u>) spread like wildfire over Chinese social media, seemingly coming out of nowhere.</p><p>In some ways, the popularity of the essay in China is comparable to the recent hype over Alex Tizonâs essay â<a href="https://www.theatlantic.com/magazine/archive/2017/06/lolas-story/524490/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">My Familyâs Slave</a>â on Western social media; this non-fiction story about &#8216;Lola&#8217; Eudocia Tomas Pulido from the Philippines, who lived as a modern slave with an American family for 56 years, went viral on Twitter and Facebook in May. It gripped its many readers for exposing poignant problems in modern-day society that usually stay behind closed doors.</p><p>Fan Yusu&#8217;s account, in its own way, also revealed the harsh realities of an ever-changing society. China has an estimated 282 million rural migrant workers. The autobiographical tale focuses on the difficult childhood and adult life of one person amidst these 282 million &#8211; Fan Yusu herself.</p><div id="attachment_11101" style="width: 310px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img class="size-full wp-image-11101" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODdhAQABAPAAAP///wAAACwAAAAAAQABAEACAkQBADs=" data-lazy-src="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/04/61add7e3ly1ff04fct3nvj20qe0hmn27-e1493206350886.jpg" alt="" width="300" height="200" /><noscript><img class="size-full wp-image-11101" src="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/04/61add7e3ly1ff04fct3nvj20qe0hmn27-e1493206350886.jpg" alt="" width="300" height="200" /></noscript><p class="wp-caption-text">F<em>an Yusu on April 25, 2017. Photo by Sina Finance.</em></p></div><p>&#8220;I Am Fan Yusu&#8221; was first published on <a href="http://www.weibo.com/noonstory?refer_flag=1001030101_" target="_blank">Noonstory.com</a>, an online literary platform by Shanghai news outlet <a href="http://www.jiemian.com/article/1275141.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Jiemian</a>. A year prior to its publication, one of their journalists (<a href="http://www.sohu.com/a/136533704_550958" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Dan Bao æ·¡è±¹</a>) headed out to Picun for an interview. Picun is a migrant village on the outskirts of Beijing, not far from the capital&#8217;s airport. It was here that Jiemian learned about the local literary club and its many poems and essays. One of these works titled &#8220;My Brother&#8217;s Dream&#8221; particularly stood out to them. It was written by the 44-year-old Fan Yusu, and the editor soon decided to publish her first story (&#8220;åæ°å¤§å¥&#8221;) in May of 2016.</p><p>A year later, Jiemian published a second essay by Fan, not expecting that it was this piece that would soon hit three million views and go viral across the country.</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h4><strong>HER STORY</strong></h4><blockquote><p><em><span style="font-size: 35px;">&#8220;Am I living in the Qing dynasty or is this new socialist China?&#8221;</span></em></p></blockquote><p>&nbsp;</p><p>In &#8220;<a href="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/fan-yusu-%e6%88%91%e6%98%af%e8%8c%83%e9%9b%a8%e7%b4%a0-full-translation/" target="_blank">I Am Fan Yusu</a>&#8220;, Fan recounts her impoverished childhood in a rural village in Xiangyang, Hebei. Fan came to Beijing at the age of 20. Being a clumsy waitress with low expectations for her future, she rushed into an unhappy marriage with an alcoholic husband and had two daughters. When her marriage turned violent, she returned to her hometown for help. It was here that she discovered she was &#8220;merely a passer-by&#8221; in the village where she was born and raised. Her brothers could, but were not willing to help; her mother wanted to, but could not help.</p><p>Being the youngest of five siblings, Fan already learned at a young age that men have the final say in China&#8217;s countryside. Although her mother was a powerful local politician for more than forty years, she resigned from her official post the moment Fan&#8217;s oldest brother objected to his mother&#8217;s work.</p><p>The twenty-something Fan then returned to Beijing and took on a job as a nanny in a rich family, leaving her own two daughters behind in the village of Picun, where many other children grow up without a mother. She writes about life as a babysitter for the child of her boss&#8217;s mistress, while his dressed-up young wife waits on the sofa every night for her husband&#8217;s return: &#8220;I wondered if I was living in the Qing dynasty or if this was new socialist China.&#8221;</p><div id="attachment_11565" style="width: 551px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODdhAQABAPAAAP///wAAACwAAAAAAQABAEACAkQBADs=" data-lazy-src="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/05/98fe75a2gw1fb43j1y6oaj20go0b375f-541x360.jpg" alt="" width="541" height="360" class="size-large wp-image-11565" /><noscript><img src="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/05/98fe75a2gw1fb43j1y6oaj20go0b375f-541x360.jpg" alt="" width="541" height="360" class="size-large wp-image-11565" /></noscript><p class="wp-caption-text"><em>Gate at village of Picun, Beijing.</em></p></div><p>Despite her low educational background, Fan always had a thirst for knowledge and became a well-read person with a love for writing and literature. âIf a person cannot feel happiness or satisfaction in life,&#8221; she writes: &#8220;they simply arenât reading enough novels.&#8221;</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h4><strong>ONE VOICE OUT OF MILLIONS</strong></h4><blockquote><p><em><span style="font-size: 35px;">&#8220;We are all Fan Yusu.&#8221;</span></em></p></blockquote><p>&nbsp;</p><p>Thousands of netizens shared Fan&#8217;s essay shortly after it was published online. They responded to it with praise, saying it was a &#8220;unique piece of work&#8221; and even &#8220;Nobel-Prize worthy.&#8221; Why did specifically this essay become so hyped on Chinese social media?</p><p>The answer can be found in both the person of Fan Yusu herself as in her essay. The piece appealed to people because it uses simple yet powerful language. Some called it &#8220;unpolished&#8221;; a reflection of Fan&#8217;s own life and society at large.</p><p>The fact that Fan Yusu is in her forties, a single mother of two, and a migrant worker who has had a difficult life, makes her story carry more weight. She represents a voice that is generally lost in a media environment that is dominated by the middle class.</p><p>The popularity of this account also shows that a migrant worker with a low educational status can still be a successful writer. At a 2015 social gathering, Fan already mentioned that &#8220;&#8216;migrant worker&#8217; is not a derogatory term, just as &#8216;artist&#8217; is not an elite one.&#8221;</p><p>&#8220;I Am Fan Yusu&#8221; also touches upon numerous issues such as domestic violence, divorce, gender inequality, the poor and rich divide, and a lacking healthcare system. These being issues that a lot of people have to deal with, the catchphrase &#8220;We are all Fan Yusu&#8221; (&#8220;æä»¬é½æ¯èé¨ç´ &#8221;) soon made its rounds on WeChat and Weibo.</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h4><strong>A NEW LITERARY MOVEMENT?</strong></h4><blockquote><p><em><span style="font-size: 35px;">&#8220;Many in China&#8217;s elite literary circles do not touch upon society&#8217;s pain points the way Fan does.&#8221;</span></em></p></blockquote><p>&nbsp;</p><p>Fan Yusu&#8217;s account comes at a time when there is a surge of stories that tell the individual stories of ordinary people. An essay on Beijing&#8217;s crazy housing market titled &#8220;Housing Madness&#8221; (&#8220;æ¿ç¯&#8221;) by an author named Chongzi (è«å­) also saw its fair share of success in April of this year.</p><p>There is a <a href="http://www.sixthtone.com/news/661/reality-check-china%20s-growing-appetite-nonfiction" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">growing appetite</a> for these types of stories, and non-fiction websites such as Noonstory or Guyu Story provide a platform for them.</p><p>The popularity of such stories seems to relate to a growing weariness with established literature. On Weibo, many people shared their overall discontent with China&#8217;s literary circles in response to Fan&#8217;s essay. Many said they think of Chinese literature as being elitist and out of touch with &#8216;real life.&#8217;</p><div id="attachment_11541" style="width: 450px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img class="size-full wp-image-11541" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODdhAQABAPAAAP///wAAACwAAAAAAQABAEACAkQBADs=" data-lazy-src="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/05/00635H42ly1ffffdyoaoej30go0b4n31.jpg" alt="" width="440" height="293" data-lazy-srcset="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/05/00635H42ly1ffffdyoaoej30go0b4n31.jpg 440w, https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/05/00635H42ly1ffffdyoaoej30go0b4n31-300x200.jpg 300w, https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/05/00635H42ly1ffffdyoaoej30go0b4n31-230x153.jpg 230w" data-lazy-sizes="(max-width: 440px) 100vw, 440px" /><noscript><img class="size-full wp-image-11541" src="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/05/00635H42ly1ffffdyoaoej30go0b4n31.jpg" alt="" width="440" height="293" srcset="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/05/00635H42ly1ffffdyoaoej30go0b4n31.jpg 440w, https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/05/00635H42ly1ffffdyoaoej30go0b4n31-300x200.jpg 300w, https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/05/00635H42ly1ffffdyoaoej30go0b4n31-230x153.jpg 230w" sizes="(max-width: 440px) 100vw, 440px" /></noscript><p class="wp-caption-text"><em> Renowned author Zheng Shiping (Yefu) praised Fan&#8217;s writings in a recent interview.</em></p></div><p>This idea was backed by renowned novelist Zheng Shiping (a.k.a. Yefu éå¤«), who applauded Fan&#8217;s writing in <a href="http://video.caixin.com/2017-05-04/101086280.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">an interview </a>in May. He criticized Chinese modern literature, saying that many in those &#8220;elite circles&#8221; never touch upon society&#8217;s pain points in the way Fan does.</p><p>Some Weibo netizens <a href="http://www.weibo.com/1663937380/F1zQxF0Kf?refer_flag=1001030106_&amp;type=comment#_rnd1496149291957" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">responded with sarcasm</a>, saying: &#8220;Literature is literature. No matter if it touches upon society&#8217;s pain points or not, it always needs to follow the ideology of the Propaganda Department.&#8221;</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h4><strong>ROOTLESS WOMEN OF CHINA</strong></h4><blockquote><p><em><span style="font-size: 35px;">&#8220;A married daughter is like water that has been poured.&#8221;</span></em></p></blockquote><p>&nbsp;</p><p>Another major factor that has contributed to Fan&#8217;s sudden success, is that her account shows the <a href="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/farmer-girl-city-rootless-women-chinas-countryside/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">disadvantaged position of women in China&#8217;s countryside</a>. Rural women are often caught in a vulnerable position, facing various economic and social obstacles that hinder their emancipation.</p><p>&#8220;A married daughter is like water that has been poured,&#8221; is a saying about countryside women who go out to marry. They often leave the house empty-handed. Fan addresses this &#8216;floating life&#8217; of rural women in her essay. Women from the countryside are &#8216;rootless&#8217; because their status, location, and economic rights change depending on the role they have as daughter, bride, wife, daughter-in-law, or mother. All land ownership is generally in name of the fathers, husbands, and sons (<em>Also see <u><a href="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/farmer-girl-city-rootless-women-chinas-countryside/" target="_blank">this article</a></u> on China&#8217;s &#8216;rootless women&#8217;</em>).</p><p>When a woman marries outside her <em>hukou</em> (household registration permit), she usually has to give up any benefits or rightful land ownership she had in her previous household. No matter if a woman gets married into a different household or joins China&#8217;s mass urbanization, she often is bound to end up in the lowest layers of society.</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h4><strong>WHAT HAPPENED TO FAN YUSU?</strong></h4><blockquote><p><em><span style="font-size: 35px;">&#8220;Why is Fan Yusu censored?&#8221;</span></em></p></blockquote><p>&nbsp;</p><p>Only three days after Fan Yusu&#8217;s essay went online and viral, the text disappeared from its original source [<em>editor&#8217;s note: the essay is still available on <a href="http://news.qq.com/a/20170425/063100.htm" target="_blank">some websites</a></em>]. Different Chinese media reported that Fan Yusu, overwhelmed by the media&#8217;s attention, had gone into hiding in a mountainous village.</p><p>It was not just Fan&#8217;s essay, but also its reviews that were soon &#8220;harmonized&#8221; (è¢«åè°, meaning &#8216;censored&#8217;). One popular Weibo blog titled &#8220;Why I Like Fan Yusu&#8221; was no longer accessible as of May 6 for &#8220;violating the rules&#8221; on Weibo. The sudden disappearance of the essay and its direct reviews also made many netizens wonder: &#8220;Why is Fan Yusu censored? (èé¨ç´ æä¹è¢«åè°äº?)&#8221;</p><p>Although the real reasons are not exposed, there is ample speculation. In her account, Fan writes about her problems with social anxiety. The sudden attention for her personal life may have been so overwhelming that some suggested it is Fan herself who wanted her essay removed. Especially since there were also journalists who went to her Hebei hometown to interview her mother &#8211; something that she dreaded. &#8220;Iâve run into a sandstorm,&#8221; Fan Yusu <a href="https://news.cgtn.com/news/3d51444f7a597a4d/share_p.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">reportedly</a> told her friend about the flock of journalists swarming into her village.</p><p>But there were also those who said that reasons for censorship perhaps related to the fact that the account revealed details about the personal life of her former boss, a rich and powerful man who may have put a halt to online publications.</p><p>Another plausible option is that the publication was removed due to its criticism on Chinese society and politics. &#8220;Sharp criticism is just not allowed,&#8221; some people commented: &#8220;She is very realistic, and exposes some gloomy aspects [of society].&#8221; Although Chinese state media initially lauded Fan&#8217;s essay, it is possible that the hype surrounding it just grew too big too fast.</p><p>The sudden rise and disappearance of Fan Yusu has some resemblance to the hype surrounding Chai Jing and her documentary &#8220;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Under_the_Dome_(film)" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Under the Dome</a>&#8221; in 2015. This self-funded documentary on China&#8217;s pollution problem originally was supported by Chinese state media. It received over 200 million views before it was abruptly removed from Chinese websites a week after its release.</p><p>At the time, Greenpeace East Asia&#8217;s Calvin Quek told <a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2015-03-12/china-hails-then-bans-antipollution-film-under-the-dome" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Bloomberg </a> that it might had to do with the timing, just before the start of China&#8217;s <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lianghui" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">plenary sessions</a>: &#8220;Itâs a reflection of some kind of political infighting that they chose to shut it down. The government censored the film because it got 200 million views, and they did not want it to dominate the twin conferences,&#8221; he said.</p><p>In Fan&#8217;s case, the hype came just before the Beijing <em>One Belt, One Road</em> Summit, a very significant event during and around which Chinese media <a href="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/chinas-belt-road-propaganda-machine-runs-full-speed-overview/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">emphasized</a> the idea of China as a responsible and harmonious global leader.</p><p>Although Fan Yusu&#8217;s &#8216;sandstorm&#8217; has gradually blown over by now, she still has not returned to her Picun home according to the latest media reports. Fan might have disappeared from the limelight for now, she is not forgotten.</p><div id="attachment_11554" style="width: 550px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><img class="size-large wp-image-11554" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODdhAQABAPAAAP///wAAACwAAAAAAQABAEACAkQBADs=" data-lazy-src="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/05/116610-1-540x360.jpg" alt="" width="540" height="360" /><noscript><img class="size-large wp-image-11554" src="https://www.whatsonweibo.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/05/116610-1-540x360.jpg" alt="" width="540" height="360" /></noscript><p class="wp-caption-text"><em> Worker&#8217;s culture home in Picun (by Southern Weekend / åæ¹å¨æ«).</em></p></div><p>Fan Yusu is the voice of a social class often ignored; she is a shining example that migrant workers can influence and shape the world of Chinese literature today. According to research by the <a href=\"https:\/\/www.whatsonweibo.com\/chinas-post-1990s-job-hopping-generation\/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">Mycos Institute<\/a>, only 40% of China&#8217;s post-90s workers stay in their job for longer than two years. Within a time frame of three years, 8% has four or more different jobs.<\/p>\n<p>What is the main problem of the post-90 workers that leads to <a href=\"https:\/\/www.whatsonweibo.com\/chinas-post-1990s-job-hopping-generation\/\" rel=\"noopener\" target=\"_blank\">all this job-hopping<\/a> and sleepless nights? Is the pressure on China&#8217;s job market too much to handle for this only-child generation?<\/p>\n<p>&#8220;The biggest obstacle to overcome for me and most of my friends is the fact that there&#8217;s a big gap between what we imagined our work to be and what the reality is,&#8221; Yue Xin tells What&#8217;s on Weibo. Yue did her Bachelor&#8217;s at a Shanghai college and recently completed a master&#8217;s degree in liberal arts at a British university.<\/p>\n<p>&#8220;I read on Weibo the other day that, unlike the post-70s and post-80s generation, China&#8217;s young job-seekers prioritize personal happiness and freedom over anything else,&#8221; she says: &#8220;For many of us, a long-term contract does not feel like a reassurance but like a constraint, which might prevent us from taking on more exciting challenges. It is not all about the money &#8211; it is about following our passion.&#8221;<\/p>\n<p>&nbsp;<\/p>\n<h4><strong>CHINA&#8217;S PRICELESS CHILDREN<\/strong><\/h4>\n<blockquote><p><em><span style=\"font-size: 35px;\">&#8220;Around 8 million university graduates enter China&#8217;s job market each year.&#8221;<\/span><\/em><\/p><\/blockquote>\n<p>&nbsp;<\/p>\n<p>The high job expectations among China&#8217;s post-90s workers relate to their position in their families and in society at large. As described by Liu Fengshu (2015) in &#8220;The Rise of the &#8216;Priceless&#8217; Child in China,&#8221; the so-called <em>jiulinghou<\/em> is a one-child generation that was born amidst the rapid socio-economic changes of post-Mao China, which saw a dramatic growth of both wealth and technological developments.<\/p>\n<p>Parents and grandparents have both pampered and pressured these children; not just because it takes more than a high school education to succeed in a society that is changing so quickly, but also because the urban post-90s generation was the first to have access to education and career opportunities in a way their (grand)parents never had. They are therefore also often called the &#8216;lucky generation&#8217; (\u5e78\u8fd0\u7684\u4e00\u4ee3).<\/p>\n<div id=\"attachment_15480\" style=\"width: 460px\" class=\"wp-caption aligncenter\"><img class=\"size-full wp-image-15480\" src=\"data:image\/gif;base64,R0lGODdhAQABAPAAAP\/\/\/wAAACwAAAAAAQABAEACAkQBADs=\" data-lazy-src=\"https:\/\/www.whatsonweibo.com\/wp-content\/uploads\/2017\/10\/s4_5462f2c280cad.jpg\" alt=\"\" width=\"450\" height=\"290\" data-lazy-srcset=\"https:\/\/www.whatsonweibo.com\/wp-content\/uploads\/2017\/10\/s4_5462f2c280cad.jpg 450w, https:\/\/www.whatsonweibo.com\/wp-content\/uploads\/2017\/10\/s4_5462f2c280cad-300x193.jpg 300w\" data-lazy-sizes=\"(max-width: 450px) 100vw, 450px\" \/><noscript><img class=\"size-full wp-image-15480\" src=\"https:\/\/www.whatsonweibo.com\/wp-content\/uploads\/2017\/10\/s4_5462f2c280cad.jpg\" alt=\"\" width=\"450\" height=\"290\" srcset=\"https:\/\/www.whatsonweibo.com\/wp-content\/uploads\/2017\/10\/s4_5462f2c280cad.jpg 450w, https:\/\/www.whatsonweibo.com\/wp-content\/uploads\/2017\/10\/s4_5462f2c280cad-300x193.jpg 300w\" sizes=\"(max-width: 450px) 100vw, 450px\" \/><\/noscript><p class=\"wp-caption-text\">Editorial cartoon from Chinese media. Left is the post-70s who says |I have to work overtime.&#8221; In the middle the post-80s generation saying: &#8220;I won&#8217;t work overtime.&#8221; On the right the post-90 generation saying: &#8220;I won&#8217;t work at all.&#8221;<\/p><\/div>\n<p>But all this parental investment has also set the bar high for the future. Around 8 million new university graduates now enter China&#8217;s job market each year, but their chances of finding a job that suits their education and personal expectations are slim. <\/p>\n<p>With all their high hopes and graduate diplomas, they are facing a job market mismatch. They often have no working experience and, as they have often spent years studying before entering the employment market, they are not willing to take on jobs with low education requirements.<\/p>\n<p>&nbsp;<\/p>\n<h4><strong>&#8216;LUCKY&#8217; BUT STRESSED<\/strong><\/h4>\n<blockquote><p><span style=\"font-size: 35px;\"><em>&#8220;If any of these requirements are not there, it will be very stressful for us.&#8221;<\/em><\/span><\/p><\/blockquote>\n<p>&nbsp;<\/p>\n<p>Is the <em>jiulinghou<\/em> being unreasonable in what they expect from their work? A Beijing post-90er nicknamed &#8216;Pedy&#8217;, who teaches Chinese to foreigners at a Chaoyang educational institution, does not think so. She tells What&#8217;s on Weibo: &#8220;We all just want a job that (1) we find interesting, (2) suits our skills, (3) offers a reasonable salary, (4) is located not too far from where we live, and (5) gives us a sense of success or achievement. If any of these requirements are not there, it will be very stressful for us.&#8221;<\/p>\n<p>&#8216;Pedy&#8217; mentions the cost of life in China&#8217;s bigger cities as one of the major problems: &#8220;The salaries of people just starting out on the job market are generally quite low, while the cost of living in cities like Beijing is very high. But if you live too far from your company, it means spending long and tiring hours crammed in public transport every morning and every evening.&#8221;<\/p>\n<p><img class=\"aligncenter size-large Unafraid of losing a job they do not care much for anyway, many of these post-90ers are those who have become known for quitting their job because of the &#8216;cold weather&#8217; or other seemingly random reasons.<\/p>\n<p>&nbsp;<\/p>\n<h4><strong>THE SO-CALLED &#8216;DREAM JOB&#8217;<\/strong><\/h4>\n<blockquote><p><em><span style=\"font-size: 35px;\">&#8220;If we do not finish our projects in time, they will subtract an amount from our wages.&#8221;<\/span><\/em><\/p><\/blockquote>\n<p>&nbsp;<\/p>\n<p>Perhaps the biggest challenge faced by China&#8217;s well-educated, urban post-90s workers who are entering the employment market is the troubling Catch 22 situation at hand: they will be stressed and pressured if they do not find that top job, but when they do, they are often also stressed and pressured.<\/p>\n<p>When scoring a much-desired job at one of the top companies (such as one of the &#8216;Big Four&#8217; firms Deloitte, Ernst &#038; Young, PricewaterhouseCoopers\/PwC and KPMG), young workers will often do all they can to keep their job. These jobs come with relatively high salaries and future possibilities to higher positions, but they also go hand in hand with long hours and unpaid overwork. Those who refuse to work overtime will be labeled as &#8216;non-ambitious&#8217; or &#8216;not loyal enough.&#8217;<\/p>\n<p>Beijing resident Li Jiang has a good job that suits his skills, is not too far from home, has a good salary, and offers prospects for further growth. Despite his stable contract, he is used to working over hours and often does not come home until late at night. <\/p>\n<p>&#8220;We do not get paid for overtime,&#8221; Li says: &#8220;But they just give us too much work to handle. If we do not finish our projects in time, they will subtract an amount from our wages, although this is not noted in my contract.&#8221;<\/p>\n<p>Although (illegal) overtime may endanger workers&#8217; health due to the excessive long working hours, it is still commonplace in China (Kim &#038; Chung 2016). Over recent years, some stories of young professionals literally working themselves to death &#8211; also known by the Japanese term &#8216;karoshi&#8217; &#8211; have made headlines. <\/p>\n<p>In 2011, the story of the 25-year-old PwC auditor Pan Jie <a href=\"http:\/\/shanghaiist.com\/2011\/04\/15\/25-year-old_shanghai_auditor_worked_to_death.php\" rel=\"noopener\" target=\"_blank\">went viral<\/a> on Sina Weibo when doctors <a href=\"http:\/\/ascportfolios.org\/chinaandmedia\/2011\/04\/13\/a-auditor-lady-in-pwc-died-from-overwork\/\" rel=\"noopener\" target=\"_blank\">concluded<\/a> that her overwork at the company might have played a crucial role in her death. <\/p>\n<p>Likewise, the behind-the-desk death of a <a href=\"http:\/\/adage.com\/article\/global-news\/young-ogilvy-china-staffer-s-death-raises-issue-overwork\/241500\/\" rel=\"noopener\" target=\"_blank\">24-year-old Ogilvy employee <\/a>in Beijing and the <a href=\"http:\/\/www.chinadaily.com.cn\/china\/2016-06\/30\/content_25920247.htm\" rel=\"noopener\" target=\"_blank\">2016 death of Jin Bo<\/a>, a deputy editor-in-chief of one of China&#8217;s leading online forums, all prompted calls for increased public awareness on the risks of overwork &#8211; especially amongs young professionals. <\/p>\n<p>Despite these headlines, Li continues working over hours: &#8220;Perhaps it&#8217;s in our culture. Nobody wants to be the one leaving first or sticking their heads out to ask about employee rights. <em>Meiyoubanfa<\/em>, it&#8217;s just the way it is.&#8221;<\/p>\n<p>Yue Xin from Shanghai has something different in mind for her future. She has received job offers from several companies, but despite &#8220;feeling flattered,&#8221; none of them met her expectations. She is not lazy or fickle, she says; she is currently just looking for more interesting opportunities and is &#8220;following her passions&#8221; in Europe.<\/p>\n<p><em>By <a href=\"http:\/\/www.twitter.com\/manyapan\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Manya Koetse<\/a><\/em><br \/>\n<a class=\"twitter-follow-button\" href=\"https:\/\/twitter.com\/whatsonweibo\" data-show-count=\"false\">Follow @whatsonweibo<\/a><script async src=\"\/\/platform.twitter.com\/widgets.js\" charset=\"utf-8\"><\/script><\/p>\n<p>References<\/p>\n<p>Fengshu Liu. 2016. &#8220;The Rise of the \u201cPriceless\u201d Child in China.&#8221; <em>Comparative Education Review<\/em> 60 (1): 105-130.<\/p>\n<p>Kim, S., &#038; Chung, S. 2016. &#8220;Explaining Organizational Responsiveness to Emerging Regulatory Pressure: The Case of Illegal Overtime in China.&#8221; <em>The International Journal of Human Resource Management<\/em> 27(18): 2097\u20132118. <\/p>\n<p><em>Spotted a mistake or want to add something?
