Their backgrounds are diverse. They are the sons and daughters of a former factory owner, restaurateurs, a doctor, a construction worker, a local official, and a school administrator. </p><p data-reactid="52">They attended three different universities – China&rsquo;s leading trade and economics college in Beijing, a regional university in the central city of Wuhan, and a third-tier technology school in the southwestern city of Chengdu. </p><p data-reactid="53">That makes them somewhat privileged in a country where relatively few people get to pursue higher education. </p><p data-reactid="54">Their experiences differ, often widely. And yet, they have much in common. </p><p data-reactid="55">They are optimistic, open-minded and tend to have a free-spirited streak, even though family roots and obligations remain important. For the unmarried among them, some feel acute pressure to find a mate. Most have had everything they&rsquo;ve needed - food and clothing - and often more, things like Nintendo Gameboys or vacations. </p><p data-reactid="58">They&rsquo;ve all known extraordinary economic growth year in and year out, making a painful slowdown hard to imagine. Property prices only rise in their world – a double edged sword for young adults trying to get established. </p><p data-reactid="59">China&rsquo;s upward trajectory is a given. </p><p data-reactid="60">&ldquo;I think the world is getting better and better,&rdquo; said Qin Lijuan, 28, a personal finance consultant in Chengdu. &ldquo;Even if we face an economic crisis, as long as you make safe plans your life is still worry-free.&rdquo; </p><div class="Image_container_1tVQo" data-reactid="61"><figure tabindex="-1" data-reactid="62"><div class="LazyImage_container_1Z7A0" style="background-image:none;" data-reactid="63"><div class="LazyImage_image_1KsYy LazyImage_fallback_2kFne" style="background-image:url(//s4.reutersmedia.net/resources/r/?m=02&amp;d=20171017&amp;t=2&amp;i=1205834315&amp;r=LYNXMPED9G0W8&amp;w=20);background-position:center center;" data-reactid="64"></div></div></figure><figcaption data-reactid="65"><span class="Image_caption_KoNH1" data-reactid="66">Zhang Weixuan, an assistant secretary of the board at a software company, poses for pictures near her office in Beijing&#x27;s financial district, China, September 3, 2017.   REUTERS/Thomas Peter</span></figcaption></div><p data-reactid="67">Members of the &ldquo;Class of 2012&rdquo;, like many in China, project political apathy – reflecting perhaps instinctual caution on a sensitive topic in the presence of journalists, or possibly genuine insouciance. </p><p data-reactid="68">&ldquo;I don&rsquo;t care about politics because my job has nothing to do with politics,&rdquo; said Zheng Yue, 27, an interior designer in Chengdu. &ldquo;And also I can&rsquo;t solve political problems. Even if I cared a lot about it, it&rsquo;s no use. I can&rsquo;t change anything.&rdquo; </p><p data-reactid="69">When political issues are injected into their daily lives, as with internet censorship, they always find a workaround so they can see their favorite television shows or news stories they&rsquo;re interested in. </p><h3 data-reactid="70">BUBBLY OPTIMISM </h3><p data-reactid="71">Xi faces deep socio-economic challenges, and has made it clear that he believes more government control -
