		<div class="story-content" itemprop="articleBody">
		<p>In 1889, in honour of his 60th birthday King Oscar II of Sweden offered a rich prize to any scientist who could solve an ancient puzzle known as the Three Body Problem.</p>
<p>It was infuriatingly simple. If you know the mass, location, and velocity of three hypothetical objects, such as planets or moons, how can you predict the future path of their orbits?</p>
<p>The answer, as the mathematician Henri Poincar&eacute; explained in the essay which won him the king&rsquo;s prize, is that you cannot &mdash; at least not with the old-fashioned algebra and calculus known to him at the time. Despite a minor scandal over an error in Poincar&eacute;&rsquo;s paper, that is basically where things stood for many years.</p>
<figure id="attachment_" style="width:640px;" class="wp-caption post-img size_this_image_test aligncenter" itemprop="associatedMedia" itemscope itemid="photo url" itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject"><img src="http://wpmedia.nationalpost.com/2017/10/3226-9.jpg?w=640&amp;quality=60&amp;strip=all" class="wp-image-76885864" srcset="http://wpmedia.nationalpost.com/2017/10/3226-9.jpg?w=640&amp;quality=60&amp;strip=all 640w, http://wpmedia.nationalpost.com/2017/10/3226-9.jpg?w=1280&amp;quality=60&amp;strip=all 1280w, http://wpmedia.nationalpost.com/2017/10/3226-9.jpg?w=150&amp;quality=60&amp;strip=all 150w, http://wpmedia.nationalpost.com/2017/10/3226-9.jpg?w=300&amp;quality=60&amp;strip=all 300w, http://wpmedia.nationalpost.com/2017/10/3226-9.jpg?w=768&amp;quality=60&amp;strip=all 768w, http://wpmedia.nationalpost.com/2017/10/3226-9.jpg?w=1024&amp;quality=60&amp;strip=all 1024w" sizes="(max-width: 640px) 100vw, 640px" /><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">
<span class="img-caption">
Henri Poincare ( 1854-1912 ), French mathematician</span>
<span class="img-author">

<span itemprop="creator">Roger Viollet/Getty Images</span></span>

</figcaption>

</figure>
<p>Now, however, Chinese scientists have used one of the world&rsquo;s fastest supercomputers to discover nearly 700 new unique and specific solutions to the Three Body Problem, according to their new paper in the journal Science China Physics Mechanics &amp; Astronomy. Until now, there had only been about a dozen, mostly discovered over the last few years, each representing an orbit pattern that repeats and never leads to collision. There may be an infinite number of these solutions still waiting to be found, but there is no general solution that works in all cases.</p>
<p>Neither Poincar&eacute; nor the king knew it at the time, but the birthday contest sparked the first inkling of chaos theory, one of the three great scientific breakthroughs of the 20th century, along with quantum mechanics and relativity.</p>
<p>Two centuries earlier, in 1687, Isaac Newton had discovered how gravity works, and explained the mathematics of how it applies to two planets, varying according to the distance between them. That was a relatively simple calculation, known as an inverse square law. But he realized that if you add a third planet into the system, the math breaks down. The equations that describe the movement of the three bodies becomes what mathematicians call non-linear. The old rules do not apply. Hence, the Three Body Problem.</p>
<figure id="attachment_" style="width:238px;" class="wp-caption post-img size_this_image_test alignright" itemprop="associatedMedia" itemscope itemid="photo url" itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject"><img src="http://wpmedia.nationalpost.com/2017/10/ma_newtom.jpg?w=238&amp;quality=60&amp;strip=all" class="wp-image-76885873" srcset="http://wpmedia.nationalpost.com/2017/10/ma_newtom.jpg?w=238&amp;quality=60&amp;strip=all 238w, http://wpmedia.nationalpost.com/2017/10/ma_newtom.jpg?w=476&amp;quality=60&amp;strip=all 476w, http://wpmedia.nationalpost.com/2017/10/ma_newtom.jpg?w=119&amp;quality=60&amp;strip=all 119w" sizes="(max-width: 238px) 100vw, 238px" /><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">
<span class="img-caption">
Isaac Newton had discovered how gravity works, and explained the mathematics of how it applies to two planets</span>
<span class="img-author">

<span itemprop="creator">Archive photo</span></span>

</figcaption>

</figure>
<p>It took a while for scientists and philosophers to come to terms with this shift. They had always assumed that the world worked by physical laws, and if you could measure initial conditions accurately enough, those laws would let you predict the future indefinitely. As James Gleick described it in his book Chaos: Making a New Science, this view was very wrong.</p>
<p>&ldquo;There was always one small compromise, so small that working scientists usually forgot it was there, lurking in a corner of their philosophies like an unpaid bill. Measurements could never be perfect,&rdquo; he wrote. &ldquo;Scientists marching under Newton&rsquo;s banner actually waved another flag that said something like this: Given an approximate knowledge of a system&rsquo;s initial conditions and an understanding of natural law, one can calculate the approximate behaviour of the system. This assumption lay at the philosophical heart of science.&rdquo;</p>
<p>Today we know how wrong this assumption was. The Three Body Problem is now recognized as a classic example of a chaotic system. Like the butterfly that causes a hurricane by flapping its wings, it is exquisitely sensitive to initial conditions. The tiniest tweak can have massive consequences down the line.</p>
<p>Predicting these orbits requires a new style of calculation that was not developed until the 20th century, and even then, only about a dozen repeating patterns were ever discovered, and only in the last few years. In most scenarios, the math shows that the Three Bodies would just keep on whirling around chaotically, never repeating their paths.</p>
<p>In some cases, when one of the three bodies has a very small mass, the orbits of the other two can create what are known as Lagrangian Points, locations where the gravity of the two larger objects is in perfect balance, and the smaller third object is held in a stable location. This is of great use in astronomy, for example space-based telescopes, which can be placed at the Lagrangian Points of the Earth and Moon, or Earth and Sun.</p>

<li><a href="http://nationalpost.com/news/canada/u-s-gets-canadian-help-to-take-on-china-in-supercomputer-race-a-perfect-world-for-d-wave">U.S. gets Canadian help to take on China in supercomputer race: ‘A perfect world for D-Wave’</a></li>

<li><a href="http://nationalpost.com/news/for-the-first-time-china-dominates-list-of-worlds-fastest-supercomputers">For the first time, China dominates list of world&#8217;s fastest supercomputers</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Like the endlessly repeating patterns of chaos theory, the new solutions discovered by the Chinese researchers make for elaborate and weirdly beautiful images when they are plotted in two dimensions. They are unlikely to have ever existed in reality, however. Because of how solar systems form, planets, moons and stars tend to settle into regular orbits on a single plane.</p>
<p>The Chinese researchers credit their discoveries to advancements in computer science and their novel technique called clean numerical simulation, which is a strategy for modelling chaotic systems, in which solutions are reached indirectly by continuous refinement, rather than directly by brute calculation.</p>
<p>&ldquo;In this paper, we numerically obtain 695 families of Newtonian periodic planar collisionless orbits of three-body system with equal mass and zero angular momentum in case of initial conditions with isosceles collinear configuration, including the well-known figure-eight family found by Moore in 1993, the 11 families found by &Scaron;uvakov and Dmitra&scaron;inović in 2013, and more than 600 new families that have never been reported, to the best of our knowledge,&rdquo; reads the report by XiaoMing&nbsp;Li and ShiJun&nbsp;Liao.</p>
