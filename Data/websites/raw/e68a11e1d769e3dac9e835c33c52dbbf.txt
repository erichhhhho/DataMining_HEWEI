<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="291" data-total-count="2148">China’s ties with two regional heavyweights — Japan and India — remain strained, and Mr. Xi faces an unusually <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2017/09/05/world/asia/north-korea-nuclear-weapons-china.html">precarious situation on the Korean Peninsula</a>, with both the North and the South defying him, one building a nuclear arsenal and the other deploying American missile defenses.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="378" data-total-count="2526">Still, in a <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2017/10/18/world/asia/china-xi-jinping-party-congress.html">marathon opening speech</a> last week, Mr. Xi showed no sign of retreat and hinted at even bigger spending to vault China to world greatness: <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2017/10/11/world/asia/xi-jinping-military-china-purge.html">more for the military</a> to make it a first-class fighting outfit with global reach, and more for his overseas infrastructure program, <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2017/05/13/business/china-railway-one-belt-one-road-1-trillion-plan.html">the “One Belt, One Road” initiative</a>, which he sees as a way to win friends around the world.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="268" data-total-count="2794">Deng Xiaoping, the architect of China’s economic reforms that started in the late 1970s, set a policy of keeping a low profile in international affairs and biding time. But more than his predecessors, Mr. Xi is abandoning that approach — and encountering pushback.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="250" data-total-count="3044">In Germany, a sharp increase in Chinese investment has prompted complaints that China is closing its markets even as it goes on a buying spree abroad, especially of valuable technology companies. Policy makers are considering options for retaliating.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="211" data-total-count="3255">There are also concerns that China is trying to divide the European Union by <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2017/08/26/world/europe/greece-china-piraeus-alexis-tsipras.html">cultivating poorer countries like Hungary and Greece</a> and using them to block policies supported by richer countries that hurt Beijing.</p><div id="story-ad-2" class="story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent ">

But in China’s case, that pushback comes not just from the West but also from neighbors who remember the tributary system of its imperial past — or are wary of its Communist political system despite its embrace of capitalism decades ago.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="242" data-total-count="3776">Mr. Xi has made inroads in Southeast Asia, gaining influence in Myanmar, Malaysia and Thailand.
